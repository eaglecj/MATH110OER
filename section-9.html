Section 3.4 Subspace, Basis, Dimension, and Rank

Exercises Exercises

1.

(Source: [3], Chapter 5, Exercise 5.3.1)

Which of the following sets are subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^3? \) Explain.
  1. \(\displaystyle V_1 = \left\{u = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ u_3 \end{bmatrix} \ \Bigg| \ |u_1| \le 4 \right\}. \)
  2. \(\displaystyle V_2 = \left\{u = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ u_3 \end{bmatrix} \ \Bigg| \ u_i \ge 0 \ \text{for each } i = 1,2,3 \right\}. \)
  3. \(\displaystyle V_3 = \left\{u = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ u_3 \end{bmatrix} \ \Bigg| \ u_3 + u_1 = 2u_2 \right\}. \)
  4. \(\displaystyle V_4 = \left\{u = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ u_3 \end{bmatrix} \ \Bigg| \ u_3 \ge u_1 \right\}. \)
  5. \(\displaystyle V_5 = \left\{u = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ u_3 \end{bmatrix} \ \Bigg| \ u_3 = u_1 =0 \right\}. \)

2.

(Source: [3], Chapter 4, Exercise 4.10.58)

Suppose \(V \) and \(W \) are subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^n \text{.}\) Let \(V \cap W \) be all vectors which are in both \(V \) and \(W \text{.}\) Show that \(V \cap W \) is a subspace also.

3.

(Source: [4], Chapter 5, Exercise 5.1.24)

Show that every proper subspace \(U \) of \(\mathbb{R}^2 \) is a line through the origin. Hint: If \(\mathbf{d} \) is a nonzero vector in \(U \) , let \(L = \mathbb{R}\textbf{d} = \{ r\textbf{d} \ | \ r \text{ in } \mathbb{R}\} \) denote the line with direction vector \(\mathbf{d}. \) If \(\mathbf{u} \) is in \(U \) but not in \(L \text{,}\) argue geometrically that every vector \(\mathbf{v} \) in \(\mathbb{R}^2 \) is a linear combination of \(\mathbf{u} \) and \(\mathbf{d}. \)

4.

(Source: [3], Chapter 5, Exercise 5.5.1)

Determine the rank and nullity and find a basis of the column space, row space, and null space of each of the following matrices.
  1. \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 9 \amp 6 \\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
  2. \begin{equation*} B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 3 \amp 0 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 9 \amp 1 \amp 7 \\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 1 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
  3. \begin{equation*} C = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 3 \\ 3 \amp 1 \amp 10 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 4 \\ 1 \amp -1 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
  4. \begin{equation*} D = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp -2 \amp -18\\ 1 \amp 2 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 11 \\ -1 \amp -2 \amp -2 \amp 1 \amp 11 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
  5. \begin{equation*} E = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 3 \amp 0 \\ 3 \amp 1 \amp 10 \amp 0 \\ -1 \amp 1 \amp -2 \amp 1\\ 1 \amp -1 \amp 2 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

5.

(Source: [4], Chapter 5, Exercise 5.2.3)

Find a basis and calculate the dimension of the following subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^4 \text{.}\)
  1. \begin{equation*} \text{span}\{ (1,-1,2,0), (2,3,0,3), (1,9,-6,6)\} \end{equation*}
  2. \begin{equation*} \text{span}\{(2,1,0,-1), (-1,1,1,1), (2,7,4,1)\} \end{equation*}
  3. \begin{equation*} \text{span}\{(-1,2,1,0), (2,0,3,-1), (4,4,11,-3), (3, -2, 2, -1)\} \end{equation*}
  4. \begin{equation*} \text{span}\{(-2,0,3,1), (1,2,-1,0), (-2,8,5,3), (-1, 2, 2, 1)\} \end{equation*}

6.

(Source: [4], Chapter 5, Exercise 5.4.3)

  1. Can a \(3 \times 4 \) matrix have independent columns? Independent rows? Explain.
  2. If \(A \) is \(4 \times 3 \) and rank \(A \) =2, can \(A \) have independent columns? Independent rows? Explain.
  3. If \(A \) is an \(m \times n \) matrix and rank \(A = m \text{,}\) show that \(\le n \text{.}\)
  4. Can a nonsquare matrix have its rows independent and its columns independent? Explain.
  5. Can the nullspace of a \(3 \times 6 \) matrix have dimension 2? Explain.
  6. Suppose that \(A \) is \(5 \times 4 \) and null\((A) = \mathbb{R}x \) for some column \(\mathbf{x}\ne 0 \text{.}\) Can \(\dim( \text{Im}A)=2? \)
