Section 1.1 The Geometry and Algebra of Vectors

Exercises Exercises

1.

(Source: [3], Section 2.1, Exercise 2.1.2)

Given the points \(P = (2,0,4)\) and \(Q= (5,-2,1)\text{,}\) find \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\) and \(\overrightarrow{QP}\text{.}\)
Hint 1
By definition, the components of \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\) are the difference of the respective components of the point \(Q\) and of the point \(P\text{.}\)
Hint 2
Be careful with the order: for example, \(\vect{PQ}\) has first component \(q_1 - p_1 = 5 - 2 = 3\text{.}\)
Answer
\begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{PQ} = \begin{bmatrix} 3\\ -2\\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \text{ and } \overrightarrow{QP} = \begin{bmatrix} -3\\ 2\\ 3 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
Solution
The components of \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\) are the difference of the respective components of the point \(Q\) and of the point \(P\text{.}\) Therefore,
\begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{PQ} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 - 2\\ -2 - 0\\ 1-4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3\\ -2\\ -3 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
Similarly,
\begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{QP} = \begin{bmatrix} 2-5\\ 0-(-2) \\ 4-1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -3\\ 2\\ 3 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
Notice that \(\overrightarrow{PQ} = (-1)\overrightarrow{QP}\text{.}\)

2.

(Source: [4], Section 4.1, Exercise 4.1.10)

In each case, find a point \(Q\) such that \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\) has
  1. the same direction as \(\mathbf{v}\text{;}\)
  2. the opposite direction of \(\mathbf{v}\text{.}\)
  1. \(P=(-1,2,2)\text{,}\) \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\)
  2. \(P=(3,0,-1)\text{,}\) \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \)
Hint 1
The vector \(\vect{PQ}\) can be computed as
\begin{equation*} \vect{PQ} = \vect{OQ}-\vect{OP}, \end{equation*}
where \(O\) denotes the origin.
Hint 2
A vector \(\mbf{w}\) has the same direction as \(\mbf{v}\) if \(\mbf{w} = c \mbf{v}\) for some positive real number \(c\text{.}\) It has the opposite direction if \(\mbf{w} = c \mbf{v}\) for some negative real number \(c\text{.}\)
Hint 3
For \(c=1\text{,}\) what does Hint 2 tell you about \(\vect{PQ}\text{?}\) Can you then use Hint 1 to find \(Q\) for Part i?
Answer
There are multiple correct answers, for example:

  1. same direction: \(Q = (0 , 5 , 3).\)

    opposite direction: \(Q = (-2 , -1 , 1).\)

  2. same direction: \(Q = (5 , -1 , 2).\)

    opposite direction: \(Q = (1 , 1 , -4).\)

Solution
First, recall that the vector \(\vect{PQ}\) can be computed as
\begin{equation*} \vect{PQ} = \vect{OQ}-\vect{OP}, \text{ where } O \text{ denotes the origin.} \end{equation*}
Since we are looking for a point \(Q\text{,}\) we should rephrase this equation:
\begin{equation} \vect{OQ} = \vect{OP} + \vect{PQ}.\label{ex-vec-betw-pts-eq-vec-OQ}\tag{1.1.1} \end{equation}
Secondly, recall that \(\vect{PQ}\) has the same (resp. the opposite) direction of \(\mbf{v}\) if \(\vect{PQ} = c \mbf{v}\) for some positive (resp. negative) real number \(c\text{.}\) Plugging this back into (1.1.1), we see that we would like \(Q\) to satisfy
\begin{equation*} \vect{OQ} = \vect{OP} + c\mbf{v} \end{equation*}
for some positive (resp. negative) number \(c\text{.}\) Therefore, we should compute \(\vect{OP}+\mbf{v}\) (resp. \(\vect{OP}-\mbf{v}\)) for the given point \(P\) and the given vector \(\mbf{v}\text{.}\) 1 

First, our solution for \(P=(-1,2,2)\) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\):

same direction: We compute

\begin{equation*} \vect{OP}+\mbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1+1 \\ 2+3 \\ 2+1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 5 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

We have argued before that this should equal \(\vect{OQ}\text{,}\) so we conclude:

\begin{equation*} Q = (0 , 5 , 3). \end{equation*}

Let us check our solution: we have

\begin{equation*} \vect{PQ} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 5 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0-(-1) \\ 5-2 \\ 3-2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \mbf{v}, \end{equation*}

so that \(\vect{PQ}\) indeed has the same direction as \(\mbf{v}\) (since they are equal).

opposite direction: We compute

\begin{equation*} \vect{OP}-\mbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1-1 \\ 2-3 \\ 2-1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

We have argued before that this should equal \(\vect{OQ}\text{,}\) so we conclude:

\begin{equation*} Q = (-2 , -1 , 1). \end{equation*}

Let us check our solution: we have

\begin{equation*} \vect{PQ} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2-(-1) \\ -1-2 \\ 1-2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ -3 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = (-1) \mbf{v}, \end{equation*}

so that \(\vect{PQ}\) indeed has the opposite direction as \(\mbf{v}\text{.}\)

Next, our solution for \(P=(3,0,-1)\) and \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \text{:}\)

same direction: We compute

\begin{equation*} \vect{OP}+\mbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3+2 \\ 0+(-1) \\ -1+3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

We have argued before that this should equal \(\vect{OQ}\text{,}\) so we conclude:

\begin{equation*} Q = (5 , -1 , 2). \end{equation*}

Let us check our solution: we have

\begin{equation*} \vect{PQ} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5-3 \\ -1-0 \\ 2-(-1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \mbf{v}, \end{equation*}

so that \(\vect{PQ}\) indeed has the same direction as \(\mbf{v}\) (since they are equal).

opposite direction: We compute

\begin{equation*} \vect{OP}-\mbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3-2 \\ 0-(-1) \\ -1-3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

We have argued before that this should equal \(\vect{OQ}\text{,}\) so we conclude:

\begin{equation*} Q = (1 , 1 , -4). \end{equation*}

Let us check our solution: we have

\begin{equation*} \vect{PQ} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ -4\end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1-3 \\ 1-0 \\ -4-(-1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} = (-1) \mbf{v}, \end{equation*}

so that \(\vect{PQ}\) indeed has the opposite direction as \(\mbf{v}\text{.}\)

Notice that we could also compute \(\vect{OP}+5\mbf{v}\text{,}\) \(\vect{OP}+ \pi \mbf{v}\text{,}\) or many other things for Item 1.1.2.i, but the positive number \(+1\) is an easy choice. Similarly, for Item 1.1.2.ii, we could also compute \(\vect{OP}-5\mbf{v}\text{,}\) \(\vect{OP} - \frac{1}{8} \mbf{v}\text{,}\) etc. - there are many correct answers.

3.

(Source: [2], Chapter II, Section "Vectors in Space", Problem 2)

Decide if the two vectors are equal.
  1. The vector from \((5,3)\) to \((6,2)\) and the vector from \((1,-2)\) to \((1,1)\text{.}\)
  2. The vector from \((2,1,1)\) to \((3,0,4)\) and the vector from \((5,1,4)\) and \((6,0,7)\text{.}\)
Hint
It might be helpful to look back at Hint 1.1.1.1 and Hint 1.1.1.2.
Answer
  1. The two vectors are different.
  2. The two vectors are the same.
Solution
  1. By definition, the vector \(\vect{v}\) from \((5,3)\) to \((6,2)\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \vect{v}=\begin{bmatrix} 6 - 5 \\ 2 - 3 \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
    Similarly, the vector \(\vect{w}\) from \((1,-2)\) to \((1,1)\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \vect{w}=\begin{bmatrix} 1 - 1 \\ 1 - (-2) \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
    Since \(\vect{v}\) and \(\vect{w}\) do not have the same components, we conclude that they are different.
  2. By definition, the vector \(\vect{v}\) from \((2,1,1)\) to \((3,0,4)\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \vect{v}=\begin{bmatrix} 3-2 \\ 0 - 1 \\ 4 - 1 \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
    Similarly, the vector \(\vect{w}\) from \((5,1,4)\) and \((6,0,7)\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \vect{w}=\begin{bmatrix} 6 - 5 \\ 0 - 1 \\ 7 - 4 \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
    Since \(\vect{v}\) and \(\vect{w}\) have the same components, we conclude that they are the same.

4.

(Source: [4], Section 4.1, Exercise 4.1.8)

Let \(\mathbf{p}\) and \(\mathbf{q}\) be the vectors of points \(P\) and \(Q\text{,}\) respectively, and let \(R\) be the point whose vector is \(\mathbf{p+q}\text{.}\) Express the following in terms of \(\mathbf{p}\) and \(\mathbf{q}\text{:}\)
  1. \(\overrightarrow{QP}\text{,}\)
  2. \(\overrightarrow{QR}\text{,}\)
  3. \(\overrightarrow{RP}\text{,}\)
  4. \(\overrightarrow{RO}\) where \(O\) is the origin.
Hint
If \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are the vectors of points \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) respectively, and if \(O\) denotes the origin, then by definition \(\vect{AB} = \vect{OB}-\vect{OA} = \mbf{b}-\mbf{a}\text{.}\)
Answer
  1. \begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{QP} = \mbf{p}-\mbf{q}. \end{equation*}
  2. \begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{QR} = \mbf{p}. \end{equation*}
  3. \begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{RP} = -\mbf{q}. \end{equation*}
  4. \begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{RO} = -\mbf{p} - \mbf{q}. \end{equation*}
Solution
In the following, let \(\mbf{r}\) be the vector of the point \(R\text{.}\) In other words,
\begin{equation*} \mbf{r} = \mbf{p}+\mbf{q} \end{equation*}
by the choice of \(R\text{.}\)
  1. By definition, the components of \(\overrightarrow{QP}\) are the difference of the respective components of the point \(P\) and of the point \(Q\text{,}\) or in other words:
    \begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{QP} = \mbf{p}-\mbf{q}. \end{equation*}

  2. Similarly, we have

    \begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{QR} = \mbf{r}-\mbf{q} = (\mbf{p}+\mbf{q}) -\mbf{q} = \mbf{p}. \end{equation*}

  3. Just as above, we have

    \begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{RP} = \mbf{p}-\mbf{r} = \mbf{p}-(\mbf{p}+\mbf{q}) = -\mbf{q}. \end{equation*}

  4. If we write \(\mbf{0}\) for the vector of the origin \(O\text{,}\) all of whose components are zero, then

    \begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{RO} = \mbf{0} - \mbf{r} = - (\mbf{p}+\mbf{q}) = -\mbf{p} - \mbf{q}. \end{equation*}

5.

(Source: [3], from Section 2.2, Exercise 2.2.1.)

Find
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
Hint
Vector addition is defined componentwise. For example,
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5+(-2) \\ 6+3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
Answer
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 9 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
Solution
By definition of vector addition, we have to add the numbers componentwise, so:
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1+1+(-1) \\ 2+5+2 \\ 3+1+(-4) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 9 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

6.

(Source: [3], from Section 2.3, Exercise 2.3.1)

Draw the vectors \(\mathbf{u} =\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \) and \(\mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \text{.}\) Then draw \(-\mbf{u}, 2\mbf{v}, -1/2\mbf{v}, \mbf{u}+\mbf{v},\) and \(-\mbf{u}+\mbf{v}\) on the same set of coordinate axes.
Solution

We choose to have all seven vectors anchored at the origin.

Notice that \(\mbf{u}+\mbf{v}\) represents the diagonal of the parallelogram with sides \(\mbf{u}\) and \(\mbf{v}\text{,}\) and similarly, \(-\mbf{u}+\mbf{v}\) represents the diagonal of the parallelogram with sides \(-\mbf{u}\) and \(\mbf{v}\text{.}\) Furthermore, \(-1/2\mbf{v}\) has the opposite direction of \(\mbf{v}\text{,}\) and \(-\mbf{u}\) the opposite direction of \(\mbf{v}\text{.}\)

7.

(Source: [4], from Section 4.1, Exercise 4.1.30.)

Let \(A, B, C, D, E,\) and \(F\) be the vertices of a regular hexagon, taken in order. Show that
\begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{AB} + \overrightarrow{AC} + \overrightarrow{AD} + \overrightarrow{AE}+ \overrightarrow{AF} = 3\overrightarrow{AD} . \end{equation*}
Hint 1
If \(X\) denotes the barycentre of the hexagon, what is the relationship between \(\vect{AB}\text{,}\) and \(\vect{XA}\) and \(\vect{XB}\text{?}\)
Hint 2
If \(X\) denotes the barycentre of the hexagon, what is the relationship between \(\vect{XA}\) and \(\vect{XD}\text{?}\) What about \(\vect{XB}\) and \(\mbf{e}\text{?}\)
Solution
Denote by \(X\) the barycentre of the hexagon. By construction,
\begin{equation*} \vect{XA} = -\vect{XD},\; \vect{XB} = -\mbf{e},\; \vect{XC} = -\vect{XF}. \end{equation*}
Now, by definition \(\vect{AB} = \vect{OB}-\vect{OA}\text{,}\) where \(O\) denotes the origin. If we add and substract \(\vect{OX}\) on the right hand-side, we do not change the equality, so
\begin{equation*} \vect{AB} = \vect{OB}-\vect{OA} + \vect{OX} - \vect{OX} = (\vect{OB} - \vect{OX}) - (\vect{OA} - \vect{OX}) = \vect{XB} - \vect{XA}. \end{equation*}
For convenience, let us denote \(\vect{XA}, \ldots, \vect{XF}\) by \(\mbf{a}, \ldots, \mbf{f}\text{.}\) We can now rewrite the left-hand side of the equation in question as follows:
\begin{align*} \amp \overrightarrow{AB} + \overrightarrow{AC} + \overrightarrow{AD} + \overrightarrow{AE}+ \overrightarrow{AF} \\ \amp\quad = (\mbf{b}-\mbf{a}) + (\mbf{c}-\mbf{a}) + (\mbf{d}-\mbf{a}) + (\mbf{e}-\mbf{a}) + (\mbf{f}-\mbf{a}) \amp\amp\\ \amp\quad = (\mbf{b}+\mbf{d}) + (\mbf{c}+\mbf{d}) + (\mbf{d}+\mbf{d}) + (\mbf{e}+\mbf{d}) + (\mbf{f}+\mbf{d}) \amp\amp \text{ since } \mbf{a} = -\mbf{d}\\ \amp\quad = \mbf{b} + \mbf{c} + \mbf{e} + \mbf{f} + 6 \mbf{d} \amp\amp \end{align*}
Since \(\mbf{b}=-\mbf{e}\) and \(\mbf{c}=-\mbf{f}\text{,}\) we conclude:
\begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{AB} + \overrightarrow{AC} + \overrightarrow{AD} + \overrightarrow{AE}+ \overrightarrow{AF} = 6 \mbf{d} . \end{equation*}
Now, since \(\mbf{a}=-\mbf{d}\text{,}\) we can write \(2\mbf{d}=\mbf{d} - \mbf{a}\text{,}\) so that
\begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{AB} + \overrightarrow{AC} + \overrightarrow{AD} + \overrightarrow{AE}+ \overrightarrow{AF} = 3(\mbf{d} - \mbf{a}) = 3\vect{AD}, \end{equation*}
as claimed.

8.

(Source: [3], from Section 2.3, Exercise 2.2.2.)

Use the properties of vector addition to show the following equalities. Justify every step.
  1. \(\displaystyle (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})+\mathbf{w} =(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})+\mathbf{u}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{0})+(\mathbf{v}+(-\mathbf{u})) =\mathbf{v}.\)
Hint 1
Recall the properties of vector addition: for any vectors \(\mbf{x}, \mbf{y}, \mbf{z}\) with the same number of components, and \(c,d\) any real numbers, we have
\begin{align} \text{Associativity:} \amp\amp \mbf{x} + (\mbf{y} + \mbf{z}) \amp = (\mbf{x} + \mbf{y}) + \mbf{z}\label{axioms-vector-addition-associativity}\tag{1.1.2}\\ \text{Commutativity:} \amp\amp \mbf{x} + \mbf{y} \amp = \mbf{y} + \mbf{x}\label{axioms-vector-addition-commutativity}\tag{1.1.3}\\ \text{Neutral element } \mbf{0} \text{ of addition}: \amp\amp \mbf{0} + \mbf{x} \amp = \mbf{x}\label{axioms-vector-addition-neutral-elt}\tag{1.1.4}\\ \text{Existence of inverse elements:} \amp\amp \mbf{x} + (-\mbf{x}) \amp = \mbf{0}\label{axioms-vector-addition-inverses}\tag{1.1.5}\\ \text{Identity of scalar multiplication:} \amp\amp 1\mbf{x} \amp = \mbf{x}\label{axioms-vector-addition-scalar-1}\tag{1.1.6}\\ \text{Compatibility:} \amp\amp c(d\mbf{x}) \amp = (cd)\mbf{x}\label{axioms-vector-addition-compatibility}\tag{1.1.7}\\ \text{Distributivity 1:} \amp\amp c(\mbf{x} + \mbf{y}) \amp = c\mbf{x} + c\mbf{y}\label{axioms-vector-addition-distributivity-1}\tag{1.1.8}\\ \text{Distributivity 2:} \amp\amp (c+d)\mbf{x} \amp = c\mbf{x} + d\mbf{x}\label{axioms-vector-addition-distributivity-2}\tag{1.1.9} \end{align}
Hint 2
For this question, you will only need the following axioms: Associativity (1.1.2), Commutativity (1.1.3), Neutral element (1.1.4), Inverse element (1.1.5).
Solution
  1. \begin{align*} (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})+\mathbf{w} \amp = \mathbf{u}+(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}) \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-associativity.html}{\text{Associativity (1.1.2)}})\\ \amp = (\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w})+\mathbf{u} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-commutativity.html}{\text{Commutativity (1.1.3)}}) \end{align*}

  2. First, notice that

    \begin{align*} \mathbf{u}+\mathbf{0} \amp = \mathbf{0}+\mathbf{u} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-commutativity.html}{\text{Commutativity (1.1.3)}})\\ \amp = \mathbf{u} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-neutral-elt.html}{\text{Neutral element (1.1.4)}}) \end{align*}

    Therefore,

    \begin{align*} (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{0})+(\mathbf{v}+(-\mathbf{u})) \amp = \mathbf{u}+(\mathbf{v}+(-\mathbf{u})) \amp \text{ (above argument)}\\ \amp = \mathbf{u}+((-\mathbf{u})+\mathbf{v}) \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-commutativity.html}{\text{Commutativity (1.1.3)}})\\ \amp = (\mathbf{u}+(-\mathbf{u}))+\mathbf{v} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-associativity.html}{\text{Associativity (1.1.2)}})\\ \amp = \mathbf{0}+\mathbf{v} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-inverses.html}{\text{Inverse element (1.1.5)}})\\ \amp = \mathbf{v} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-neutral-elt.html}{\text{Neutral Element (1.1.4)}}) \end{align*}

9.

(Source: [3], from Section 2.3, Exercise 2.3.3.)

Use the properties of vector addition to show the following equalities, where \(k, l\) are scalars. Justify every step.
  1. \((k+l)(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}) = k \mathbf{u} +k \mathbf{v} + l\mathbf{u} + l \mathbf{v}\text{.}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle 0 \mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0}\)
  3. \(\displaystyle (-1)\mathbf{u} =-\mathbf{u}\)
  4. \(\displaystyle -(k\mathbf{u}) = k(-\mathbf{u}) =(-k)\mathbf{u}\)
Hint 1
It might help to look back to Hint 1.1.8.1.
Hint 2
Once you have proved one of the statements, you may use it to prove another statement.
Solution
  1. \begin{align*} (k+l)(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}) \amp = (k+l)\mathbf{u}+ (k+l)\mathbf{v} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-distributivity-1.html}{\text{Distributivity 1 (1.1.8)}})\\ \amp = k\mathbf{u}+l\mathbf{u}+ k\mathbf{v}+l\mathbf{v} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-distributivity-2.html}{\text{Distributivity 2 (1.1.9)}})\\ \amp = k \mathbf{u} +k \mathbf{v} + l\mathbf{u} + l \mathbf{v} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-commutativity.html}{\text{Commutativity (1.1.3)}}) \end{align*}

    Notice that one could also first have used Distributivity 2 (1.1.9) and only then Distributivity 1 (1.1.8).

  2. First, we compute:

    \begin{align*} 0 \mathbf{u} \amp = (0 \cdot 2)\mathbf{u} \amp \text{ because } 0=0\cdot 2\\ \amp = 0 ( 2 \mathbf{u}) \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-compatibility.html}{\text{Compatibility (1.1.7)}})\\ \amp = 0 (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{u}) \amp \text{(Definition)}\\ \amp = 0\mathbf{u} + 0\mathbf{u} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-distributivity-2.html}{\text{Distributivity 2 (1.1.9)}}) \end{align*}

    To sum up, we have proved:

    \begin{equation*} 0 \mathbf{u} = 0\mathbf{u} + 0\mathbf{u} \end{equation*}

    If we add \(- 0\mathbf{u}\) on both sides, we get:

    \begin{equation*} 0 \mathbf{u} +(-0\mathbf{u}) = (0\mathbf{u} + 0\mathbf{u}) + (-0\mathbf{u}) \end{equation*}

    For the left-hand side, note that

    \begin{equation*} 0 \mathbf{u} +(-0\mathbf{u}) = \mbf{0} \end{equation*}

    by the axiom of the inverse element (1.1.5). For the right-hand side, we have

    \begin{align*} (0\mathbf{u} + 0\mathbf{u}) + (-0\mathbf{u}) \amp = 0\mathbf{u} + (0\mathbf{u} + (-0\mathbf{u})) \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-associativity.html}{\text{Associativity (1.1.2)}})\\ \amp = 0\mathbf{u} + \mbf{0} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-inverses.html}{\text{Inverse elements (1.1.5)}})\\ \amp = 0\mathbf{u} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-neutral-elt.html}{\text{Neutral element (1.1.4)}}) \end{align*}

    All in all, we have shown

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{0} = 0 \mathbf{u} +(-0\mathbf{u}) = (0\mathbf{u} + 0\mathbf{u}) + (-0\mathbf{u}) = 0\mbf{u}, \end{equation*}

    as claimed.

  3. We have to show that \((-1)\mathbf{u}\) behaves like the inverse elements \(-\mathbf{u}\) whose existence is claimed in this axiom (1.1.5). In other words, it suffices to show that
    \begin{equation*} \mbf{u} + (-1)\mbf{u} = \mbf{0}. \end{equation*}
    We compute:
    \begin{align*} \mbf{u} + (-1)\mbf{u} \amp = 1\mbf{u} + (-1)\mbf{u} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-scalar-1.html}{\text{Identity of scalar multiplication (1.1.6)}})\\ \amp = (1+ (-1))\mbf{u} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-distributivity-2.html}{\text{Distributivity 2 (1.1.9)}})\\ \amp = 0\mbf{u} \amp\\ \amp = \mathbf{0} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/ex-sums-of-vecs3-item-neutral.html}{\text{Item b}}) \end{align*}

  4. First of all,

    \begin{align*} -(k\mathbf{u}) \amp = (-1)(k\mathbf{u}) \amp (\knowl{./knowl/ex-sums-of-vecs3-item-inverse.html}{\text{Item c}})\\ \amp = ((-1)k)\mathbf{u} \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-compatibility.html}{\text{Compatibility (1.1.7)}}) \end{align*}

    We can now do two things: on the one hand, \((-1)k=-k\text{,}\) which proves that \(-(k\mathbf{u}) = -k \mbf{u}\text{.}\) On the other hand, \((-1)k = k(-1)\text{,}\) so that:

    \begin{align*} -(k\mathbf{u}) \amp = ((-1)k)\mathbf{u} \amp \text{ as argued above}\\ \amp = (k(-1))\mathbf{u} \amp \\ \amp = k((-1)\mathbf{u}) \amp (\knowl{./knowl/axioms-vector-addition-compatibility.html}{\text{Compatibility (1.1.7)}})\\ \amp = k(-\mathbf{u}) \amp (\knowl{./knowl/ex-sums-of-vecs3-item-inverse.html}{\text{Item c}}) \end{align*}

11.

(Source: [3], from Section 2.4, Exercise 2.4.2.)

Decide whether
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 4\\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
is a linear combination of the vectors
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{u}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 3\\ 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \text{ and } \mathbf{u}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
Hint 1
\(\mathbf{v}\) is a linear combination of \(\mathbf{u}_1\) and \(\mathbf{u}_2\) if and only if there exists real numbers \(c_1, c_2\) such that
\begin{equation*} \mbf{v} = c_1 \mathbf{u}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{u}_2 . \end{equation*}
Hint 2
Two vectors are equal if and only if they have the same components.
Answer
Yes, the vector \(\mbf{v}\) is a linear combination of the vectors \(\mbf{u}_1\) and \(\mbf{u}_2\text{.}\) To be precise,
\begin{equation*} \mbf{v} = 2 \mathbf{u}_1 + (-1) \mathbf{u}_2 . \end{equation*}
Solution
We want to find real numbers \(c_1, c_2\) such that
\begin{equation*} \mbf{v} = c_1 \mathbf{u}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{u}_2 . \end{equation*}
We compute the right-hand side:
\begin{align*} c_1 \mathbf{u}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{u}_2 \amp = c_1 \begin{bmatrix} 3\\ 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + c_2 \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} c_1 3\\ c_1 \\ -c_1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} c_2 2\\ c_2 (-2) \\ c_2 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} c_1 3 + c_2 2 \\ c_1 - c_2 2 \\ -c_1 + c_2 \end{bmatrix} . \end{align*}
We want this to equal \(\mbf{v}\text{,}\) i.e. we want
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 4\\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} c_1 3 + c_2 2 \\ c_1 - c_2 2 \\ -c_1 + c_2 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
In other words, we need all of the following to be true:
\begin{align} 4 \amp = c_1 3 + c_2 2 \notag\\ 4 \amp = c_1 - c_2 2\label{ex-lin-comb-of-vecs1-eq1}\tag{1.1.10}\\ -3 \amp = -c_1 + c_2 \notag \end{align}
The second equation (1.1.10) yields \(c_1 = 4 + c_2 2\text{,}\) which we will plug into the other two equations to get:
\begin{align} 4 \amp = (4 + c_2 2) 3 + c_2 2 \label{ex-lin-comb-of-vecs1-eq2}\tag{1.1.11}\\ -3 \amp = -(4 + c_2 2) + c_2 \label{ex-lin-comb-of-vecs1-eq3}\tag{1.1.12} \end{align}
The first equation (1.1.11) yields:
\begin{equation*} 4 = (4 + c_2 2) 3 + c_2 2 = 12 + c_2 8, \text{ i.e. } c_2 = -1, \end{equation*}
and the second equation (1.1.12) yields
\begin{equation*} -3 = -(4 + c_2 2) + c_2 = -4 -c_2, \text{ i.e. } c_2 = -1 . \end{equation*}
We got the same value for \(c_2\text{,}\) so the answer to our question is: Yes, the vector \(\mbf{v}\) is a linear combination of the vectors \(\mbf{u}_1\) and \(\mbf{u}_2\text{.}\)

For good measure, let us check that we did not make a mistake. With \(c_2=-1\text{,}\) we get \(c_1 = 4 + c_2 2 = 4 + (-1) 2 = 2\text{.}\) For these values, we compute:

\begin{equation*} c_1 \mathbf{u}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{u}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \cdot 3 + (-1) 2 \\ 2 - (-1) 2 \\ -2 + (-1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} = \mbf{v}, \end{equation*}
as claimed.

(Source: [3], from Section 2.4, Exercise 2.4.2.)

Decide whether
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 4\\ 4 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
is a linear combination of the vectors
\begin{equation*} \mathbf{u_1} = \begin{bmatrix} 3\\ 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} , \mathbf{u_2} = \begin{bmatrix} 8\\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \text{ and } \mathbf{u_3} = \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
Hint 1
\(\mathbf{v}\) is a linear combination of \(\mathbf{u}_1\text{,}\) \(\mathbf{u}_2\text{,}\) and \(\mathbf{u}_3\) if and only if there exists real numbers \(c_1, c_2, c_3\) such that
\begin{equation*} \mbf{v} = c_1 \mathbf{u}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{u}_2 + c_3 \mathbf{u}_2 . \end{equation*}
Hint 2
Two vectors are equal if and only if they have the same components.
Answer
No, the vector \(\mbf{v}\) is not a linear combination of the vectors \(\mbf{u}_1\text{,}\) \(\mbf{u}_2\text{,}\) and \(\mbf{u}_3\text{.}\)
Solution
We want to find real numbers \(c_1, c_2, c_3\) such that
\begin{equation*} \mbf{v} = c_1 \mathbf{u}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{u}_2 + c_3 \mathbf{u}_3 . \end{equation*}
We compute the right-hand side:
\begin{align*} c_1 \mathbf{u}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{u}_2 + c_3 \mathbf{u}_3 \amp = c_1 \begin{bmatrix} 3\\ 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + c_2 \begin{bmatrix} 8\\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + c_3 \begin{bmatrix} 2\\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} c_1 3\\ c_1 \\ -c_1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} c_2 8\\ 0 \\ -c_2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} c_3 2\\ c_3(-2) \\ c_3 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} c_1 3 + c_2 8 + c_3 2\\ c_1 +0 - c_3 2\\ -c_1 -c_2 +c_3 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
We want this to equal \(\mbf{v}\text{,}\) i.e. we want
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 4 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} c_1 3 + c_2 8 + c_3 2\\ c_1 +0 - c_3 2\\ -c_1 -c_2 +c_3 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
In other words, we need
\begin{align} 4 \amp =c_1 3 + c_2 8 + c_3 2\notag\\ 4 \amp = c_1 - c_3 2\label{ex-lin-comb-of-vecs2-eq1}\tag{1.1.13}\\ 4 \amp = -c_1 -c_2 + c_3\notag \end{align}
The second equation (1.1.13) yields \(c_1 = 4 + c_3 2\text{.}\) Plugging this into the other two equations, we get:
\begin{align} 4 \amp =(4 + c_3 2) 3 + c_2 8 + c_3 2\label{ex-lin-comb-of-vecs2-eq2}\tag{1.1.14}\\ 4 \amp = -(4 + c_3 2) -c_2 + c_3\label{ex-lin-comb-of-vecs2-eq3}\tag{1.1.15} \end{align}
The first equation (1.1.14) yields:
\begin{equation*} 4 =(4 + c_3 2) 3 + c_2 8 + c_3 2 =12 + c_3 6 + c_2 8 + c_3 2 =12 + c_2 8 + c_3 8, \end{equation*}
i.e. \(c_2 = -1 - c_3\text{.}\) Plugging this, in turn, into the last remaining equation (1.1.15) yields:
\begin{equation*} 4 = -(4 + c_3 2) -c_2 + c_3 = -4 - c_3 2 -(-1 - c_3) + c_3 = -3. \end{equation*}
Since this is false, we conclude: No, the vector \(\mbf{v}\) is not a linear combination of the vectors \(\mbf{u}_1\text{,}\) \(\mbf{u}_2\text{,}\) and \(\mbf{u}_3\text{.}\)
