1.

(Source: [2], Section 3.1, Exercise 3.1.1.)

Find the vector equation for the line through \((-7,6,0)\) and \((-1,1,4)\text{.}\) Then, find the parametric equations for this line.
Hint 1
The vector equation of a line \(\ell\) through points \(P\) and \(Q\) is given by
\begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \vect{OP} + t \vect{PQ} . \end{equation*}
Hint 2
The parametric equations of a line \(\ell\) are the equations for each of the coordinates in the vector equation.
Answer
The vector equation is given by
\begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} -7\\ 6 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 6\\ -5 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} , \end{equation*}
and the parametric equations by
\begin{equation*} x_1 = -7 + 6t ; x_2 = 6 - 5t ; x_3 = 4t . \end{equation*}
Solution
The vector equation of the line is given by
\begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} -7\\ 6 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t \left( \begin{bmatrix} -1\\ 1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} -7\\ 6 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \right) = \begin{bmatrix} -7\\ 6 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 6\\ -5 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}
To check that we did not make a mistake: for \(t=0\text{,}\) we are indeed at the point \((-7,6,0)\text{,}\) and for \(t=1\text{,}\) we are at the point
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} -7\\ 6 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 6\\ -5 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1\\ 1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} , \end{equation*}
the second point. For the parametric equations, we just have to rephrase the above vector equation in terms of its coordinates:
\begin{equation*} x_1 = -7 + 6t ; x_2 = 6 - 5t ; x_3 = 4t . \end{equation*}

  7. The lines through \(P=(1,0,1)\) that meet the line \(\ell\) whose vector equation is given by \(\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2\\ 0 \end{bmatrix} +t \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \) at points with distance 3 from \(Q=(1,2,0)\text{.}\)

    Solution

    First, let us find the points \(R\) on \(\ell\) that have distance \(3\) from \(Q\text{:}\) Having distance \(3\) means exactly that

    \begin{equation*} \norm{ \vect{QR} } = 3. \end{equation*}

    The fact that \(R\) is on \(\ell\) means that, for some real number \(t\text{,}\) we have

    \begin{equation*} \vect{OR} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2\\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    Thus,

    \begin{equation*} \vect{QR} = \left( \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2\\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \right) - \begin{bmatrix} 1\\2\\0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2t \\ -t\\ 2t \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    and we therefore want

    \begin{equation*} \norm{ \begin{bmatrix} 2t \\ -t\\ 2t \end{bmatrix} } = 3, \text{ i.e. } (2t)^2 + (-t)^2 + (2t)^2 = 9. \end{equation*}

    This means, we need \(9t^2=9\text{,}\) or in other words, \(t=\pm 1\text{.}\) Therefore, there are two points \(R_+, R_-\) on \(\ell\) which have distance \(3\) from \(Q\text{,}\) namely the points that satisfy

    \begin{equation*} \vect{O R_+} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2\\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + 1 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    and

    \begin{equation*} \vect{OR_-} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2\\ 0 \end{bmatrix} - 1 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 3\\ -2 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

    Any line through \(P\) can be described by the vector equation

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \vect{OP} + s \mbf{d} \end{equation*}

    for some directional vector \(\mbf{d}\text{.}\) For our line to go through \(R_+\text{,}\) we need to choose

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{d} = \vect{PR_+} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    and the vector equation for this line is then given by

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + s \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

    Its parametric form is therefore given by

    \begin{equation*} x_1 = 1+2s ; x_2 = s ; x_3 = 1+s . \end{equation*}

    For the line to go through \(R_-\) on the other hand, we need to choose

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{d} = \vect{PR_-} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 3\\ -2 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 3\\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    and the vector equation for this line is then given by

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + s \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 3\\ -3 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

    Its parametric form is then given by

    \begin{equation*} x_1 = 1-2s ; x_2 = 3s ; x_3 = 1-3s . \end{equation*}
    Answer
    There are two lines that meet the given line at distance \(3\) from \(Q\) and go through \(P\text{:}\)

    Vector equation of the first line:

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    Parametric form of the first line:

    \begin{equation*} x_1 = 1+2t ; x_2 = t ; x_3 = 1+t \end{equation*}

    Vector equation of the second line:

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 3\\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    Parametric form of the second line:

    \begin{equation*} x_1 = 1-2t ; x_2 = 3t ; x_3 = 1-3t . \end{equation*}

3.

(Source: [4], Section 4.2, Exercise 4.2.14.)

Find the normal, general, and parametric form of each of the following planes. Hint 1
To find a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) that is normal to a plane, you need to find two non-parallel vectors \(\mbf{a}\) and \(\mbf{b}\) in the plane and compute their cross product
\begin{equation*} \mbf{a}\times \mbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} a_2b_3-a_3b_2\\ a_3b_1-a_1b_3\\ a_1b_2-a_2b_1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
where \(a_i, b_i\) are the three components of \(\mbf{a}\) resp. \(\mbf{b}\text{.}\) This vector is orthogonal to both \(\mbf{a}\) and \(\mbf{b}\) and hence normal to the plane.
Hint 2
In order to determine the normal form of a plane, you need both a single point \(P\) on the plane and a normal vector \(\mbf{n}\) to the plane. Then, any point \(X=(x_1,x_2,x_3)\) on the plane can be described by the normal form \(\mbf{n}\cdot\vect{PX}=0\text{.}\)

  1. Passing through \(A=(2,1,3)\text{,}\) \(B=(1,1,3)\text{,}\) and \(C=(1,2,-3)\text{.}\)

    Solution

    To find a normal vector \(\mbf{n}\text{,}\) we need two non-parallel vectors in the plane; for example, the two vectors

    \begin{equation*} \vect{AB} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -1\\ 5 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1\\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -2\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    and

    \begin{equation*} \vect{AC} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2\\ -3 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1\\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ -6 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    would do. (If these two vectors had turned out to be parallel, then the three given points \(A, B, C\) would lie on a line, so they would not determine a plane.) A vector \(\mbf{n}\) which is orthogonal to both \(\vect{AB}\) and \(\vect{AC}\) is then given by their cross product (see HintÂ 1):

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{n} = \vect{AB} \times \vect{AC} = \begin{bmatrix} (-2) \cdot (-6) - 2 \cdot 1 \\ 2 \cdot 0 - 2 \cdot (-6) \\ 2 \cdot 1 - (-2)\cdot 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 12 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

    The normal form, \(\mbf{n}\cdot\vect{AX}=0\text{,}\) is now explicitly given by

    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 12 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x_1 - 1 \\ x_2 - 1\\ x_3 -3 \end{bmatrix} = 0 \end{equation*}

    or equivalently

    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 12 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 12 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1\\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = 28 . \end{equation*}

    We get the general form by simply computing the dot product on both sides:

    \begin{equation*} 10x_1 + 12x_2 + 2x_3 = 28. \end{equation*}

    Lastly, we compute the parametric form by first giving the vector form:

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \vect{OA} +t\vect{AB} +s\vect{AC} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1\\ 3 \end{bmatrix} +t\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -2\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} +s \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ -6 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

    The parametric form can now be read off:

    \begin{equation*} x_1 = 1+2t ; x_2 = 1-2t+s ; x_3 = 3+2t-6s. \end{equation*}
    Answer

    Normal form:

    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 12 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2}\\ x_{3} \end{bmatrix} = 28 \end{equation*}

    General form:

    \begin{equation*} 5x_1 + 6x_2 + x_3 = 14 \end{equation*}

    Parametric form:

    \begin{equation*} x_1 = 1+2t ; x_2 = 1-2t+s ; x_3 = 3+2t-6s \end{equation*}

  2. Passing through \(A=(1,-1,6)\text{,}\) \(B=(0,0,1)\text{,}\) and \(C=(4,7,-11)\text{.}\)

    Solution

    To find a normal vector \(\mbf{n}\) to the plane, we need two non-parallel vectors in the plane; for example, the two vectors

    \begin{equation*} \vect{BA} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1\\ 6 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1\\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    and

    \begin{equation*} \vect{BC} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 7\\ -11 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 0\\0\\1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 7\\ -12 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

    would do. (If these two vectors had turned out to be parallel, then the three given points \(A, B, C\) would lie on a line, so they would not determine a plane.) A vector \(\mbf{n}\) which is orthogonal to both \(\vect{BA}\) and \(\vect{BC}\) is then given by their cross product:

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{n} = \vect{BA} \times \vect{BC} = \begin{bmatrix} (-1) \cdot (-12) - 5 \cdot 7 \\ 5 \cdot 4 - 1 \cdot (-12) \\ 1 \cdot 7 - 4\cdot (-1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -23 \\ 32 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} . \end{equation*}

    The normal form, \(\mbf{n}\cdot\vect{BX}=0\text{,}\) is now explicitly given by

    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} -23 \\ 32 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x_1 - 0 \\ x_2 - 0\\ x_3 - 1 \end{bmatrix} = 0 \end{equation*}

    or equivalently

    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} -23 \\ 32 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} -23 \\ 32 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = 11 . \end{equation*}

    We get the general form by simply computing the dot product on both sides:

    \begin{equation*} -23x_1 + 32x_2 + 11x_3 = 11. \end{equation*}

    Lastly, we compute the parametric form by first giving the vector form:

    \begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \vect{OB} +t\vect{BA} +s\vect{BC} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} +t\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1\\ 5 \end{bmatrix} +s \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 7\\ -12 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

    The parametric form can now be read off:

    \begin{equation*} x_1 = t + 4s ; x_2 = -t + 7s ; x_3 = 1+ 5t - 12s. \end{equation*}
    Answer

    Normal form:

    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} -23 \\ 32 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \mbf{x} = 11 \end{equation*}

    General form:

    \begin{equation*} -23x_1 + 32x_2 + 11x_3 = 11 \end{equation*}

    Parametric form:

    \begin{equation*} x_1 = t + 4s ; x_2 = -t + 7s ; x_3 = 1+ 5t - 12s \end{equation*}

  3. Passing through \(P=(2,-3,5)\) and parallel to the plane with general equation \(3x_1-2x_2-x_3=0\text{.}\)

    Solution

    Two planes are parallel if and only if they have the same normal vectors. Therefore, we should first find a normal vector to the plane whose general equation is given: If we chose
    \begin{equation*} \mbf{n} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    then the given general form is equivalent to
    \begin{equation*} \mbf{n} \cdot\mbf{x} =0, \end{equation*}
    so \(\mbf{n}\) is indeed a normal vector. With this at hand, any point \(X=(x_1,x_2,x_3)\) on the plane whose normal form we are after can be described by \(\mbf{n}\cdot\vect{PX}=0\text{,}\) or more explicitly:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \left( \mbf{x} - \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ - 3 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \right) =0 \end{equation*}
    To get the general form, we have to compute the dot product on the left-hand side and rearrange:
    \begin{equation*} 3x_{1} - 2 x_{2} - x_{3} = 7. \end{equation*}
    For the vector form, we need two non-parallel vectors \(\mbf{u}\text{,}\) \(\mbf{v}\) which are orthogonal to \(\mbf{n}\text{,}\) i.e. they need to satisfy
    \begin{equation*} \mbf{u}\cdot \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = 0 \text{ and } \mbf{v}\cdot \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = 0. \end{equation*}
    For example,
    \begin{equation*} \mbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} \text{ and } \mbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    work. Therefore, the vector equation of the plane is
    \begin{equation*} \mbf{x} = \vect{OP} +t\mbf{u} +s\mbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2\\-3\\5 \end{bmatrix} +t\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} +s\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    and so the parametric equations are
    \begin{equation*} x_1 = 2 +2s ; x_2 = -3 + t + 3s ; x_3 = 5 -2t. \end{equation*}
    Answer

    Normal form:

    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix}= 7 \end{equation*}

    General form:

    \begin{equation*} 3x_{1} - 2 x_{2} - x_{3} = 7 \end{equation*}

    Parametric form:

    \begin{equation*} x_1 = 2 +2s ; x_2 = -3 + t + 3s ; x_3 = 5 -2t \end{equation*}

  4. Passing through \(P=(3,0,-1)\) and parallel to the plane with equation \(2x_1-x_2+x_3=3\text{.}\)

    Solution

    Two planes are parallel if and only if they have the same normal vectors. Therefore, we should first find a normal vector to the plane whose general equation is given: If we chose
    \begin{equation*} \mbf{n} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    then the given general form is equivalent to
    \begin{equation*} \mbf{n} \cdot\mbf{x} =3, \end{equation*}
    so \(\mbf{n}\) is indeed a normal vector. With this at hand, any point \(X=(x_1,x_2,x_3)\) on the plane whose normal form we are after can be described by \(\mbf{n}\cdot\vect{PX}=0\text{,}\) or more explicitly:

Solution
We follow the idea of HintÂ 1.3.6.1 and make one additional remark: The line from \(Q_1\) to \(Q_2\) is, by construction, contained in the plane since the two points are in the plane.
