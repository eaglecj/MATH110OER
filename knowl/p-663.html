For the other direction, suppose that \(\vec{v_i}\) is in \(\span(\vec{v_1}, \ldots, \vec{v_{i-1}}, \vec{v_{i+1}}, \ldots, \vec{v_k})\text{.}\) Since any linear combination of \(\vec{v_1}, \ldots, \vec{v_{i-1}}, \vec{v_{i+1}}, \ldots, \vec{v_k}\) is also a linear combination of \(\vec{v_1}, \ldots, \vec{v_k}\) (by making the coefficient of \(\vec{v_i}\) be \(0\)) to prove that \(\span(\vec{v_1}, \ldots, \vec{v_k}) = \span(\vec{v_1}, \ldots, \vec{v_{i-1}}, \vec{v_{i+1}}, \ldots, \vec{v_k})\) all we need to do is show that every linear combination of \(\vec{v_1}, \ldots, \vec{v_k}\) can be written as a linear combination where \(\vec{v_i}\) does not appear.
