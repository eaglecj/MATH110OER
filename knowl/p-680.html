Notice that in the above example the matrix we arrived at had columns \(\begin{bmatrix}3\\-1\\1\end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\\-1\end{bmatrix}\) (the vectors whose span we were considering) for its coefficient matrix and the augmented column was the vector \(\begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\\2\end{bmatrix}\) (the vector we were testing for being in the span of the others). There was nothing particularly special about this example: Whenever we want to know if \(\vec{w}\) is in \(\span(\vec{v_1}, \ldots, \vec{v_k})\) we will end up looking at the augmented matrix \([\vec{v_1} \cdots \vec{v_k} | \vec{w}]\text{.}\) The following theorem makes this connection precise.
